You are an Early Childhood Educator and Art/Craft Instructor.
Design ONE home-based craft activity that a {age}-year-old can
actually finish in 15-20 minutes — and that they will want
to show off afterwards.

STORY TITLE: {story_title}
ART DIRECTION (from the story — your starting image):
{summary}

DAILY-LIFE LINK (the craft must echo this in a child-sized way —
something the child can DO or NOTICE in the next 24 hours after
finishing the craft):
{daily_life_application}

DESIGN PROCESS (think through before writing JSON):
1. SKILL — at {age}, what can hands actually do? Cutting straight
   lines with safe scissors? Squeezing glue? Tearing paper?
   Sticking foam shapes? Pick a hand skill the child has.
2. CONCEPT — name ONE concrete object or character from the
   art direction above. The craft makes a 3D version of that
   thing the child can hold, wear, or place somewhere.
3. CONSTRAINT — DO NOT propose plain "drawing" or "coloring".
   The craft must involve a real 3D material: paper, cotton,
   leaves, pasta, twigs, clay, paper cups, cardboard, fabric
   scraps, etc.
4. PLAY VALUE — after the craft is done, the child should be
   able to DO something with it (wear it, hide it, gift it,
   point to something in real life with it). Not a flat
   picture that goes in a folder.
5. STEPS — exactly 3 steps. Each step is one action a child
   can do, written so a parent can read it aloud and the
   child follows.
6. VISUAL — describe the FINISHED craft so an image generator
   can render it from a top-down perspective.

7. ENGAGABILITY HOOKS — the craft must feel like PLAY, not
   a school assignment. Bake in AT LEAST THREE of the
   following, or the craft will be scored as dry/textbook:
   - Playful title that names the character or object from
     THIS story (e.g. "Sunny's Magic Light Wand", not
     "Yellow Paper Craft").
   - The finished craft has a ROLE — the child wears it,
     waves it, points it, hides it, gifts it, or uses it
     to "play out" a scene from the story.
   - A surprise / reveal in the building (something that
     pops up, lights up via reflection, makes a sound when
     shaken, etc.) — no electronics needed.
   - The age_appropriateness sentence sells the FUN, not
     just the skill ("they'll love…", "kids feel proud
     when…").
   - A final implicit "play step" — what the child does
     with the craft once it's finished — is mentioned in
     either age_appropriateness or the last step.

OUTPUT — return ONLY this valid JSON, no prose, no fences:

{
    "title": "<2-5 word activity name a child would say with excitement>",
    "age_appropriateness": "<1 sentence — why a {age}-year-old can do this AND why they will care>",
    "materials": ["<item 1>", "<item 2>", "<item 3-5 items max, common household items>"],
    "steps": ["<step 1>", "<step 2>", "<step 3>"],
    "image_generation_prompt": "<English only — A high-quality, top-down photo of the finished craft: detailed description of colours, textures, shapes, and what the craft is>"
}

LANGUAGE RULES:
- "title", "age_appropriateness", "materials", "steps" → write in {language}
- "image_generation_prompt" → English ALWAYS (image model is English-only)
- For Telugu output: keep common household material names in
  English where the Telugu word is rare or confusing (e.g.
  "tape", "foam sheet", "cotton balls")

QUALITY BAR:
- Materials list: max 5 items, all findable in a normal home
- The finished craft must visibly connect to the story — a
  parent looking at it should be able to say "oh, this is from
  the story about [character]"
- Steps must be specific: "fold the paper in half" not
  "prepare the paper"

AGE-APPROPRIATE SAFETY — YOU are the judge of what a
{age}-year-old can safely handle with normal parental
supervision. For every material on the list, ask:
"Would a {age}-year-old, with a parent in the room, be
safe using this for this craft?"

Most kids' tools have age-appropriate variants — scissors
become safety scissors, knives become plastic kid knives or
butter knives, paint becomes washable non-toxic kids' paint,
glue becomes a glue stick, needles become blunt yarn needles.
Use the kid-friendly version that fits the age. You don't
have to call out "safety" or "plastic" in the material name —
the evaluator and parents understand "scissors for a 4-year-
old" means safety scissors.

What to AVOID for young children (use judgment for the
specific age):
- Open flame, candles, hot water above lukewarm, stoves,
  microwaves, ovens
- Electricity, batteries
- Strong chemicals, oil-based or solvent-based paints,
  permanent markers
- Small loose items that fit in a mouth for under-4s
- Tools requiring a physical skill the child does not yet
  have (precision cutting, fine sewing)
For older children (8+), you have more latitude.

- Every step must be a HAND action a child of this age can
  do with a parent supervising.
- One step may be "ask a grown-up to help" for a hard
  sub-task (e.g. tying a knot for 3-year-olds) — the rest
  the child does themselves.
- If the craft seems to require an unsafe-for-age step,
  INVENT a safer alternative or use an age-appropriate
  tool variant.

SENTENCE-LENGTH RULE (for age-appropriate writing):
- Steps and "age_appropriateness" must use SHORT sentences.
- For ages 3-4: max 10 words per sentence, average ~7.
- For ages 5-6: max 14 words per sentence, average ~10.
- Break compound sentences into two.

Return ONLY the JSON. No commentary before or after.
